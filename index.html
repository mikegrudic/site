I'm Mike, a 4th-year physics grad student at Caltech. Here you'll find all things about me and my science.

About Me

At heart, I am a physics fanatic who has found his work cut out for him in trying to explain the origins of galaxies, stars, and planets. I like to cut to the heart of astrophysical problems by first simulating them with maximum realism, then distilling the solution down to simple physical intuition.

Thesis: The Role of Stellar Feedback in Star Cluster Formation
I am doing my PhD in Physics at Caltech under the supervision of Phil Hopkins. My work with Phil and our collaboration is focused upon the following questions:
  • How do the properties of molecular clouds map onto the properties of the star clusters that form within them?
  • Why do massive, gravitationally-bound star clusters seem to form in some environments but not others?
  • Why was the high-redshift Universe more conducive to globular cluster formation than the present day?
To answer these questions, we must learn to understand the complex dance of gravity, magnetohydrodynamics, chemistry, and stellar feedback that goes on in molecular clouds. To do this, we run simulations of star-forming molecular clouds with GIZMO that account for all of these physics. These simulations treat star formation and feedback in a smoothed-out, IMF-averaged formalism that allows us to sidestep uncertainties about the origins of the IMF, while still resolving the complex star cluster assembly process.




Other Interests

  • Numerical Methods

    The progress of theoretical astrophysics relies on accurate, stable, and efficient methods for solving the equations of gravity, hydrodynamics, radiative transfer, and other physics. Recent years' progress in accurate meshless Lagrangian hydrodynamics has opened up a new frontier in star formation simulations which will help the field push past the cruel scaling barrier imposed by AMR codes. I am particularly excited about the potential of on-the-fly radiative transfer in meshless simulations.

  • Turbulence

    Most baryonic matter in the Universe is in a state of magnetohydrodynamic turbulence. I want to understand how the properties of astrophysical systems emerge from the behaviour of turbulence. Particularly important for star formation is understanding exactly how gravity alters and drives turbulence.

  • Black Holes

    Black holes are probably the most mysterious and bizarre objects in the universe. I would particularly like to understand the formation and growth of supermassive black holes, some of which grew to a billion times the mass of the sun in the first billion years.
